# AI Regulations & Standards

Overview of the evolving regulatory landscape for AI systems, including the EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, and executive orders.

## Overview

Governments worldwide are establishing frameworks to govern AI development and deployment. Security professionals must understand these regulations to ensure compliance and inform risk decisions.

## Key Regulations

| Regulation | Jurisdiction | Focus | Status |
|------------|--------------|-------|--------|
| EU AI Act | European Union | Risk-based AI classification | In force (phased) |
| NIST AI RMF | United States | Risk management framework | Published (voluntary) |
| EO 14110 | United States | Safe, secure AI development | Executive order |
| UK AI Safety | United Kingdom | Frontier model safety | Framework |
| Canada AIDA | Canada | AI and Data Act | Proposed |
| China AI Regulations | China | Generative AI governance | In force |

## EU AI Act Risk Tiers

1. **Unacceptable risk** — Banned (social scoring, real-time biometric ID)
2. **High risk** — Strict requirements (critical infrastructure, employment)
3. **Limited risk** — Transparency obligations (chatbots, deepfakes)
4. **Minimal risk** — No specific obligations (spam filters, games)

## NIST AI RMF Core Functions

- **Govern** — Establish AI risk management culture and policies
- **Map** — Identify and contextualize AI risks
- **Measure** — Assess and monitor identified risks
- **Manage** — Prioritize and act on risks

## What This Means for Security

- High-risk AI systems require security assessments
- Mandatory incident reporting for certain AI failures
- Transparency requirements may expose attack surface
- Third-party auditing creates new compliance workstreams
- Documentation requirements for all AI system decisions
